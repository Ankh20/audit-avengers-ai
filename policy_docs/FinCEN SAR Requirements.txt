FinCEN SUSPICIOUS ACTIVITY REPORT (SAR) REQUIREMENTS
Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN)
Reference: FinCEN Form 111; 31 CFR 1020.320; BSA E-Filing System

1. WHAT IS A SAR
A Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) is a mandatory report filed with FinCEN when a financial
institution detects a transaction that may involve money laundering, fraud, terrorist
financing, structuring, or other criminal activity.

2. WHO MUST FILE SARs WITH FinCEN
  - Banks (national, state, federal savings)
  - Credit unions
  - Money services businesses (MSBs)
  - Broker-dealers
  - Mutual funds
  - Insurance companies
  - Casinos (gross gaming revenue > $1 million)
  - Futures commission merchants

3. SAR FILING THRESHOLDS
  - Banks and broker-dealers: $5,000 or more (or no minimum if terrorist financing suspected)
  - Money services businesses: $2,000 or more
  - Casinos: $5,000 or more

4. WHEN TO FILE
A SAR must be filed when the institution knows, suspects, or has reason to suspect:
  a. The transaction involves funds from illegal activity
  b. The transaction is designed to evade BSA reporting requirements (structuring)
  c. The transaction has no lawful purpose and no reasonable explanation
  d. The institution is being used to facilitate criminal activity

5. SAR FILING DEADLINES
  - Standard: 30 calendar days from the date of initial detection
  - No suspect identified: 60 calendar days from the date of initial detection
  - Continuing activity: file follow-up SARs every 90 days while activity continues
  - Imminent threat to national security or life: contact law enforcement immediately

6. HOW TO FILE A SAR
  - File electronically via BSA E-Filing System (bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov)
  - Use FinCEN Form 111 (Suspicious Activity Report)
  - Retain a copy of each SAR for 5 years from the date of filing
  - Supporting documentation must be retained and made available upon request

7. SAR NARRATIVE REQUIREMENTS
The SAR narrative must:
  - Be written in a clear, chronological, and complete manner
  - Describe who, what, when, where, why, and how the suspicious activity occurred
  - Identify the financial institution's suspicion with specific facts
  - Avoid abbreviations and jargon
  - Include the dollar amount and date(s) of suspicious transactions

8. CONFIDENTIALITY — TIPPING OFF PROHIBITION
  - Do NOT notify the subject of the SAR that a report was or will be filed
  - Do NOT disclose the existence of a SAR to any unauthorized person
  - Safe harbor protects good-faith filers from civil liability (31 USC 5318(g)(3))

9. COMMON SAR TRIGGERS AT FinCEN
  - Structuring (breaking up transactions to avoid $10,000 CTR threshold)
  - Rapid movement of funds with no apparent business purpose
  - Use of multiple accounts or shell companies
  - High-value wire transfers to/from high-risk jurisdictions
  - Unusual cash activity inconsistent with the customer's business profile
  - Cyber-enabled fraud or account takeover

10. SAR STATISTICS AND IMPORTANCE
FinCEN receives millions of SAR filings annually. SAR data is used by:
  - Federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies
  - Intelligence agencies
  - Regulatory examiners
  - International partners through the Egmont Group
