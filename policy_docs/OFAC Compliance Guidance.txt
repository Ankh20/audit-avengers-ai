OFAC COMPLIANCE GUIDANCE FOR FINANCIAL INSTITUTIONS
U.S. Department of the Treasury — Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC)
Reference: 31 CFR Parts 500–599; OFAC Compliance Framework (2019)

1. OFAC MISSION
OFAC administers and enforces U.S. economic and trade sanctions programs targeting
foreign countries, regimes, terrorists, narcotics traffickers, WMD proliferators,
and other national security threats.

2. LEGAL BASIS FOR OFAC SANCTIONS
  - International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)
  - Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA)
  - United Nations Participation Act (UNPA)
  - Various country-specific statutes (e.g., Iran Sanctions Act, North Korea Sanctions Act)

3. KEY SANCTIONS LISTS
  a. Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) List
     - Individuals and entities whose assets are blocked and with whom U.S. persons
       are generally prohibited from dealing
     - Updated regularly — institutions must screen against the current SDN List
     - Available at: ofac.treasury.gov/sdn-list

  b. Consolidated Sanctions List
     - Includes SDN List plus non-SDN lists (Foreign Sanctions Evaders, Sectoral Sanctions, etc.)

  c. Sectoral Sanctions Identification (SSI) List
     - Targets specific sectors of the Russian economy
     - Prohibits certain transactions (loans, equity) rather than all dealings

4. COMPLIANCE OBLIGATIONS
Financial institutions must:
  a. Screen all customers, transactions, and counterparties against OFAC lists
  b. Update screening systems within 24 hours of SDN List changes
  c. Block or reject prohibited transactions immediately
  d. Report blocked transactions to OFAC within 10 business days
  e. Submit annual reports of all blocked property by September 30
  f. Maintain records of all OFAC-related actions for 5 years

5. BLOCKED vs. REJECTED TRANSACTIONS
  Blocked: Property is frozen in a blocked account; reportable to OFAC within 10 days
  Rejected: Transaction is refused and returned; reportable to OFAC within 10 days

6. CIVIL MONETARY PENALTIES
  OFAC applies strict liability — knowledge of the violation is not required.
  Base penalties:
  - Most programs: greater of $364,921 per violation or twice the transaction value
  - Iran, North Korea, Syria: up to $1,000,000 per violation

  Mitigating factors: voluntary self-disclosure, cooperation, strong compliance program
  Aggravating factors: willfulness, management involvement, prior violations

7. VOLUNTARY SELF-DISCLOSURE (VSD)
  - Timely, complete, accurate VSD reduces penalty by up to 50%
  - Must be submitted in writing to OFAC's Compliance Division
  - Should describe the transaction, parties, amount, and remediation steps

8. GENERAL LICENSES
  General licenses authorize categories of transactions without individual approval.
  Published in relevant sanctions program regulations (31 CFR Parts 500–599).
  Examples: emergency medical services, journalistic activities, personal remittances

9. SPECIFIC LICENSES
  Individual transactions not covered by a general license require a specific license.
  Applications submitted through OFAC's online licensing portal.
  Processing times vary from days to months depending on complexity.

10. CURRENT MAJOR SANCTIONS PROGRAMS
  - Cuba (CACR) — comprehensive embargo
  - Iran (ISR/ITSR) — comprehensive sanctions
  - North Korea (NKSR) — comprehensive sanctions
  - Russia — sectoral and SDN-based sanctions (post-2022 expanded significantly)
  - Syria (SySR) — comprehensive sanctions
  - Venezuela — targeted sanctions on government/oil sector
