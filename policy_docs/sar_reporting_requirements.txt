SUSPICIOUS ACTIVITY REPORT (SAR) FILING REQUIREMENTS
U.S. Department of the Treasury / Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN)
Reference: 31 CFR 1020.320 (Banks), 31 CFR 1022.320 (MSBs), 31 USC 5318(g)

1. OVERVIEW
A Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) must be filed by financial institutions when they
know, suspect, or have reason to suspect that a transaction involves funds derived from
illegal activity, is designed to evade BSA requirements, lacks a lawful purpose, or
involves the use of the financial institution to facilitate criminal activity.

2. WHO MUST FILE SARs
The following institutions are required to file SARs with FinCEN:
  - Banks and savings associations (insured depository institutions)
  - Credit unions
  - Money services businesses (MSBs): money transmitters, check cashers, currency dealers
  - Brokers and dealers in securities
  - Mutual funds
  - Insurance companies
  - Casinos and card clubs
  - Loan or finance companies

3. WHEN TO FILE — MANDATORY TRIGGERS
A SAR must be filed when a transaction or attempted transaction:
  a. Involves or aggregates to $5,000 or more (for banks) or $2,000 or more (for MSBs)
     AND the institution knows, suspects, or has reason to suspect the transaction:
       - Involves funds from illegal activity (e.g., drug trafficking, fraud, terrorism)
       - Is structured or otherwise designed to evade BSA reporting requirements
       - Has no apparent lawful purpose and the institution cannot identify a reasonable
         explanation after reviewing available facts
       - Involves use of the financial institution to facilitate criminal activity

  b. Additional mandatory SAR categories regardless of dollar amount:
       - Known or suspected terrorist financing
       - Transactions involving senior foreign political figures (PEPs)
       - Cybersecurity events affecting the financial institution
       - Human trafficking proceeds

4. FILING DEADLINES
  - Standard SAR deadline: 30 calendar days from the date of initial detection of facts
    that may constitute a basis for filing a SAR
  - If no suspect is identified: deadline is extended to 60 calendar days from detection
  - Continuing activity SARs: if suspicious activity continues after the initial SAR,
    a follow-up SAR must be filed every 90 days until the activity stops

5. SAR CONTENT REQUIREMENTS
A complete SAR must include:
  a. Subject information: name, address, date of birth, SSN/TIN, occupation,
     and government-issued ID number of the suspect (if known)
  b. Suspicious activity information: date, amount, type of transaction,
     account numbers involved, and a detailed narrative
  c. Narrative section: a clear, chronological description of the suspicious
     activity, why it is suspicious, and all relevant facts and circumstances
  d. Filing institution information: name, address, contact person, and EIN

6. CONFIDENTIALITY — THE "TIPPING OFF" PROHIBITION
Financial institutions and their employees are PROHIBITED from:
  - Notifying the subject of the SAR or any person named in the SAR that a SAR
    has been filed or that a SAR investigation is underway
  - Disclosing the existence of a SAR to anyone outside the institution except
    to FinCEN, law enforcement, or financial institution supervisors
Violation of confidentiality carries civil and criminal penalties.

7. SAFE HARBOR PROTECTION
Financial institutions and their employees are protected from civil liability for
filing a SAR in good faith. This safe harbor covers both voluntary and mandatory SAR
filings under 31 USC 5318(g)(3).

8. RECORDKEEPING
Financial institutions must retain copies of all filed SARs and supporting
documentation for 5 years from the date of filing. Records must be made available
to FinCEN and law enforcement upon request.

9. COMMON SUSPICIOUS ACTIVITY INDICATORS
  - Structuring cash transactions just below reporting thresholds ($10,000)
  - Rapid movement of funds through multiple accounts with no business purpose
  - Use of shell companies or nominee accounts to obscure beneficial ownership
  - Inconsistencies between transaction activity and customer's stated business
  - Wire transfers to or from high-risk jurisdictions without clear business reason
  - Customer reluctance to provide required identification or documentation
  - Unusual patterns of cash deposits followed by immediate wire transfers

10. PENALTIES FOR FAILURE TO FILE
  - Civil penalties: Up to $25,000 per day for each violation
  - Criminal penalties: Up to $250,000 and/or 5 years imprisonment for willful violations
  - Institutions may also face regulatory sanctions including loss of charter
