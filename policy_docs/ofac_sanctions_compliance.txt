OFAC SANCTIONS COMPLIANCE PROGRAM GUIDELINES
U.S. Department of the Treasury — Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC)
Reference: 31 CFR Parts 500–599; OFAC Compliance Framework (May 2019)

1. OVERVIEW
The Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) administers and enforces economic and trade
sanctions based on U.S. foreign policy and national security goals. OFAC sanctions target
foreign countries, regimes, terrorists, international narcotics traffickers, proliferators
of weapons of mass destruction, and other threats to national security.

2. WHO MUST COMPLY
OFAC regulations apply to all U.S. persons, including:
  - U.S. citizens and permanent resident aliens wherever located
  - All persons and entities within the United States
  - All U.S.-incorporated entities and their foreign branches
  - In some programs: foreign subsidiaries of U.S. companies

3. THE SPECIALLY DESIGNATED NATIONALS (SDN) LIST
OFAC maintains the SDN List, which includes individuals and companies owned or controlled by,
or acting for or on behalf of, targeted countries. It also lists individuals, groups, and
entities, such as terrorists and narcotics traffickers, designated under programs not tied
to a specific country.

Financial institutions must screen all customers, transactions, and counterparties against
the SDN List before processing. Transactions with SDN-listed parties must be blocked or
rejected.

4. BLOCKED VS. REJECTED TRANSACTIONS
  BLOCKED transactions: The funds are frozen and held in a blocked account. This applies when
  the underlying transaction is prohibited but the property remains in U.S. jurisdiction.
  Example: Wire transfer from a Cuban national.

  REJECTED transactions: The transaction is refused and returned. This applies when the
  transaction would move funds or services to or from a sanctioned party.
  Example: A transaction where an SDN is a beneficiary.

5. REPORTING REQUIREMENTS
  Blocking Reports: Must be filed with OFAC within 10 business days of blocking property
  or funds.
  Annual Reports: All blocked property must be reported to OFAC annually by September 30.
  Rejection Reports: Must be filed within 10 business days of rejecting a transaction.

6. FIVE PILLARS OF AN EFFECTIVE SANCTIONS COMPLIANCE PROGRAM (SCP)
  Pillar 1 — Management Commitment:
    Senior management must demonstrate commitment to the SCP, including allocating adequate
    resources and establishing a culture of compliance.

  Pillar 2 — Risk Assessment:
    Organizations must conduct a comprehensive sanctions risk assessment, covering products,
    services, customers, counterparties, and geographic exposure.

  Pillar 3 — Internal Controls:
    Implement policies, procedures, and systems designed to identify, interdict, escalate,
    and report potentially prohibited transactions. Screening systems must be updated within
    24 hours of SDN List changes.

  Pillar 4 — Testing and Auditing:
    Regular testing and auditing of the SCP to identify weaknesses. Testing should be
    independent, thorough, and risk-based.

  Pillar 5 — Training:
    All relevant employees must receive OFAC compliance training appropriate to their role.
    Training must be conducted at least annually and upon material changes to sanctions programs.

7. CIVIL MONETARY PENALTIES
OFAC may impose civil monetary penalties for violations, even if the person did not know
the conduct was prohibited (strict liability standard applies in many programs).

  Base penalties range from:
  - Transaction value or $381,076 per violation (whichever is greater) for most programs
  - Up to $1,000,000 per violation for certain programs (e.g., Iran, North Korea)

  Factors that reduce penalties: Voluntary self-disclosure, cooperation, remediation
  Factors that increase penalties: Willfulness, management involvement, prior violations

8. VOLUNTARY SELF-DISCLOSURE (VSD)
OFAC strongly encourages self-disclosure of potential violations. A timely, complete, and
accurate VSD is a mitigating factor that can reduce a penalty by up to 50%.
VSDs must be submitted in writing to OFAC's Compliance Division.

9. GENERAL LICENSES VS. SPECIFIC LICENSES
  General Licenses: Authorize a category of transactions without requiring individual
  applications. Published in 31 CFR and on the OFAC website.

  Specific Licenses: Written authorization from OFAC for a particular transaction or
  category of transactions that would otherwise be prohibited. Applications submitted via
  OFAC's licensing portal.

10. KEY PROHIBITED ACTIONS (Summary)
  - Conducting business with SDN-listed individuals or entities
  - Processing transactions involving sanctioned countries (Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Syria, Russia — specific sectors)
  - Facilitating transactions by U.S. persons that would be prohibited if conducted directly
  - Approving, financing, guaranteeing, or otherwise facilitating a prohibited transaction
  - Evading or attempting to evade OFAC regulations
